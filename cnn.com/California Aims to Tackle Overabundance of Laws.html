California Aims to Tackle Overabundance of Laws

By Susan Candiotti and Gabe LaMonica, CNN
updated 4:40 AM EDT, Tue April 1, 2014
California law makers gather to discuss the obscenely large number of laws currently on the books.
STORY HIGHLIGHTS
  • Supporters are concerned that there are too many laws in California
  • Critics worry that lawlessness would send California into anarchy
  • Would-be lawyer: "I'd rather spend more time with my boyfriend"

(CNN) -- California's top law makers gathered yesterday to discuss what they consider to be a growing problem: the impossibly large number of state laws and statutes, and the negative effect this has on those who must study them.

Governor of California Jerry Brown, who has previously publically claimed to be "unaware of at least half of them," led the discussion.

"The time has come to put a stop to the unnecessary glut of laws that do nothing to move our great state forward, and only serve to stunt the growth of our citizens, our law students, and our future service people," he stated in his opening remarks.

The Governor shook hands and stood alongside House Democratic Leader Nancy Pelosi and former governor Arnold Schwarzenegger, who are both ardent in their support of the measure. Both Pelosi and Schwarzenegger also claim to be concerned with only a very small percentage of the laws.

Brown, who ran on a platform of legal consolidation and simplification, has drawn criticism from those who believe this to be the first step toward anarchy.

"A lawless society is not what California needs," said Lucas Mendelssohn, a local supporter of law in general and a member of the White Horde party. "We need a common coded understanding of our society's mores, and the more laws we have, the better understanding we'll have. Without law, we'd only become John Carpenter's Escape from L.A.."

When asked if he understood the laws, Mendelssohn stated that he did not, and that this was a sign that California needed even more laws.

Twice a year, thousands of students of the law take the California bar exam, and if they pass, are allowed to practice law in the state of California. While many pass the exam on their first try, some must take the exam multiple times, at great expense of both time and money.

California Governor Jerry Brown demands fewer laws for the state.

In the weeks prior to the discussion of streamlining California's laws, national surveys were conducted, showing that 68% of law students support an elimination of all law. An even larger 74% indicated they'd like to see the bar exam replaced with a bar crawl. 85% of law students responded affirmatively when asked if they'd still like to be paid ridiculous amounts of money, were they to be hired by a law firm in a state devoid of law entirely.

While feedback has been exceptionally positive thusfar, despite a small number of naysayers, sources say that the final bill may grow to incorporate a small number of laws that are still required. This number, currently estimated at four laws, is slated to include the bill eliminating all other laws, and perhaps some criminal law, but no contract law. Boo, contract law. Seriously, why do we still have to deal with this shi[edited for content].

One exceptionally bright and intelligent young woman (and dare we say, extremely attractive), Kissworthy Babe, was especially excited, though only briefly available for comment, as she was still studying in the event that the bill would prove unsuccessful. "I'd much rather spend more time with my boyfriend," who at press time is apparently pretty cute as well.
